## Introduction

Quantum computational advantage — the demonstration that a quantum device can perform a computation infeasible for any classical computer — represents a central milestone in quantum information science. Since Google's Sycamore experiment in 2019 (Arute et al., Nature 574, 505, 2019), multiple experimental platforms have reported quantum advantage claims, including superconducting random circuit sampling (RCS) (Wu et al., PRL 127, 180501, 2021; Gao et al., PRL 134, 090601, 2025; Google Quantum AI, Nature 638, 920, 2025), photonic Gaussian boson sampling (GBS) (Zhong et al., Science 370, 1460, 2020; Deng et al., PRL 134, 090604, 2025), and quantum annealing (King et al., Science 388, 199, 2025).

However, the history of quantum advantage is one of ongoing classical competition. Sycamore's original claim of 10,000 classical years was reduced to seconds by Pan and Zhang's tensor network methods (PRL 129, 090502, 2022). The first two generations of the Jiuzhang photonic GBS experiments were classically simulated by Bulmer et al. (Science Advances 8, eabl9236, 2022) and Oh et al. (Nature Physics 20, 1647, 2024), respectively. IBM Eagle's quantum utility claim was challenged within weeks by Tindall et al. (PRX Quantum 5, 010308, 2024) and Begusic et al. (Science Advances 10, eadk4321, 2024) using tensor networks and sparse Pauli dynamics. Each generation of quantum hardware has spurred corresponding advances in classical simulation methods, creating an accelerating cycle of claim and counter-claim.

The Jiuzhang series exemplifies this dynamic. Jiuzhang 1.0 (76 photons, 2020) was classically simulated by Bulmer et al. using loop hafnian methods. Jiuzhang 2.0 (113 photons, 2021) was challenged by Oh et al. using a tensor-network algorithm that exploits photon loss to reduce the effective quantum dimensionality of the output state. Each successive version has increased in scale: Jiuzhang 3.0 (Deng et al., PRL 134, 090604, 2025; arXiv:2304.12240) uses 144 squeezed-source modes with pseudo-photon-number-resolving detectors, reporting up to 255 photon-click events and claiming that exact classical simulation would require approximately 3.1 × 10^10 years on the Frontier supercomputer. Jiuzhang 4.0 (arXiv:2508.09092) further scales to 1024 sources and 8176 modes, explicitly designed to resist the MPS-based classical attacks that challenged earlier versions.

In this work, we systematically evaluate the classical simulability of Jiuzhang 3.0 using multiple independent methods. Our central finding is that the experiment operates below the critical photon-loss threshold identified by Oh et al. (η = 0.424 < η_c ≈ 0.538), placing it in a regime where the quantum output is dominated by thermal (classical) photons — only 3.6 of 255 detected photons (1.4%) carry quantum information. We implement five independent classical sampling methods spanning three algorithmic families (Gaussian quadrature, Fock-space truncation, and positive-P phase space), achieving quantitative agreement with the experiment's photon statistics and demonstrating that inter-mode quantum correlations are captured even by the simplest Gaussian baseline.

Our approach is distinguished by three features. First, we perform systematic cross-validation across multiple independent implementations, revealing a two-tier structure in the distribution of classical approximation errors that quantifies the effect of Fock-space truncation on click-level statistics. Second, we include an explicit negative control by applying the same methods to Jiuzhang 4.0 parameters, where they fail with 1086% deviation, confirming the method's specificity to the loss-dominated regime. Third, we contextualize our results alongside the concurrent independent work of Goodman et al. (arXiv:2604.12330, 2026), who report a positive-P phase-space sampler outperforming the Jiuzhang 3.0 experiment at 1152 modes with quadratic complexity. The convergence of our loss-exploitation framework and their phase-space approach provides orthogonal evidence for the classical simulability of this experiment.

These findings contribute to the broader understanding of the quantum advantage frontier by mapping the loss-dependent boundary of classical simulability for photonic quantum computing. While Jiuzhang 3.0 falls within the classically accessible regime, Jiuzhang 4.0 — with higher transmission and more sources — remains beyond the reach of all 10 classical methods surveyed in this work, underscoring that the race between quantum hardware and classical simulation continues at the current experimental frontier.
